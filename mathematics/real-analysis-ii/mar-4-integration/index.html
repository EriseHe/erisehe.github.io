    I. Definitions of Integrals #

    1. Geometric Motivation #

    To compute the area of the region under the curve $y = f(x)$:

    1. Form the upper and lower approximations:$$ \begin{align} U(f, P) & = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sup_{I_i} f(x) \cdot \ell(I_i) \ L(f, P) & = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \inf_{I_i} f(x) \cdot \ell(I_i) \end{align} $$
    2. Form upper and lower integrals: $$ \begin{align} \overline{S}_A f & = \inf_P U(f, P) \ \underline{S}_A f & = \sup_P L(f, P)\end{align}$$

    2. General Formulation #

    Step up: Let $f: A \to \mathbb{R}$ be a bold function on a bold set $A$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

    Goal: We want to define the volume of the region under the surface $y = f(x)$
    (or the integral $\int_A f(x)dx$).

    Step 1 - Choose a rectangle #

    Choose a rectangle
    $$ B = [a_1, b_1] \times [a_2, b_2] \times \dots \times [a_n, b_n] $$
    that contains $A$ and extend $f$ such that
    $$ f(x) = 0 \quad \text{when } x \notin A. $$ Then, Volume over $A$ = Volume over $B$ :
    $$ \int_A f(x) dx = \int_B f(x) dx. $$

    Step 2: Partition $B$ #

    Divide sides of $B$ into subintervals to obtain a partition $P$:
    a collection of smaller rectangles.

    Step 3: Form upper and lower sums #

    $$ \begin{align} U(f, P) &= \sum_{R \in P} (\sup_{R} f) V(R) \longrightarrow \text{Upper sum of $f$ w.r.t. $P$}\ L(f, P) &= \sum_{R \in P} (\inf_R f) V(R)\longrightarrow \text{Lower sum of $f$ w.r.t. $P$} \end{align} $$

    Step 4: Form upper and lower integrals #

    $$ \begin{align} \overline{\int }_A f & = \inf_P U(f, P) \ \underline{\int }_A f & = \sup_P L(f, P) \end{align} $$

    Observation: #

    $$ L(f, P) \leq \text{“real volume”} \leq U(f, P) $$

    $$ \underline{\int_A} f \leq \int_A f \leq \overline{\int_A} f $$

    [!definition|*] Riemann integrable We say that $f$ is Riemann integrable if: $$ \underline{\int_A} f = \overline{\int_A} f $$ and the integral of $f$ on $A$ is defined as:$$ \int_A f(x)dx = \int_A f $$

    Other Notations: #

    $$\int_A f$$ is also denoted as the Lebesgue integral or just the integral of $f$ over $A$.

    3. Equivalent Conditions (for $f$ to be integrable) #

    General setting:

    • $f: A \subset \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is bold
    • $A \subset B$ is bold
    • $B$ is a rectangle in $\mathbb{R}^n$
    • $f(x) = 0$ for $x \notin A$

    Riemann’s Condition #

    [!definition] Riemann’s Condition For every $\epsilon > 0$, there exists a partition $P_\epsilon$ of $B$ such that: $$ 0 \leq U(f, P_\epsilon) - L(f, P_\epsilon) < \epsilon $$

    Darboux’s Condition #

    [!definition] $\exists ,I$ s.t. $\forall,\epsilon > 0$, $\exists ,\delta > 0$ s.t:

    1. If $P$ is any partition of $B$ into rectangles $B_1, B_2, \dots, B_N$ with side length $\leq \delta$
    2. If $x_{1} \in B_1, x_{2}\in B_{2}$, …, $x \in B_N$, then we have: $$ \left| \sum_{i=1}^{N} f(x_i) V(B_i) - I \right| < \epsilon $$

    Remarks #

    • The number $I$ is the value of the integral.
    • This sum is called the Riemann Sum of $f$ with respect to $P$.
    • Interpretation: Darboux’s Condition says that when the partition is fine enough (i.e., side length $\leq \delta$), the Riemann Sum is a good approximation of the integral.

    [!theorem|*] The following conditions are equivalent:

    1. $f$ is integrable on $A$.
    2. $f$ satisfies Riemann’s Condition.
    3. $f$ satisfies Darboux’s Condition.

    Proof of the Theorem #

    Step 1: If $f$ is integrable $\Rightarrow$ Riemann’s Condition #

    Given $\epsilon > 0$, we need to find a partition $P_\epsilon$ such that: $$ U\left( f, P_\epsilon \right) - L\left( f, P_\epsilon \right) < \epsilon $$ Since: $$ \overline{\int_A} f = \inf_P U(f, P) $$

    By the definition of infimum:

    1. $\exists P_1$ such that: $$ U(f, P_1) \leq \overline{\int_A} f + \frac{\epsilon}{2} $$
    2. $\exists P_2$ such that (similarly): $$ L(f, P_2) \geq \underline{\int_A} f - \frac{\epsilon}{2} $$ Now, let: $$ P_\epsilon = P_1 \cup P_2 $$ Since $P_\epsilon$ is a refinement of both $P_1$ and $P_2$, it follows that: $$ \begin{align} U(f, P_\epsilon) \leq U(f, P_1)\ L(f, P_\epsilon) \geq L(f, P_2) \end{align} $$

    Thus,

    $$ \begin{align} U(f, P_\epsilon) - L(f, P_\epsilon) \leq U(f, P_1) - L(f, P_2) & < \left(\overline{\int_A} f + \frac{\epsilon}{2}\right) - \left(\underline{\int_A} f - \frac{\epsilon}{2}\right) \ & = \overline{\int_A} f - \underline{\int_A} f + \epsilon \ & = 0+\epsilon \quad\Longrightarrow \text{Riemann’s Condition} \end{align} $$

    Step 2: Riemann Condition $\Rightarrow$ $f$ integrable #

    By assumption, $\forall \epsilon > 0$, $\exists P_\epsilon$ such that: $$ 0 \leq U(f, P_\epsilon) - L(f, P_\epsilon) < \epsilon $$ Since $\overline{\int_A} f \leq U(f, P_\epsilon)$ and $\underline{\int_A} f \geq L(f, P_\epsilon)$, we have: $$ 0 \leq \overline{\int_A} f - \underline{\int_A} f \leq U(f, P_\epsilon) - L(f, P_\epsilon) < \epsilon $$ Thus, $$ \overline{\int_A} f = \underline{\int_A} f \Rightarrow f \text{ integrable.} $$
