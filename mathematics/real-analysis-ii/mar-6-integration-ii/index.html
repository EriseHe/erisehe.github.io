    I. Equivalent Conditions for Integrability #

    [!definition|Main] Reall Let $f: A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ be a bounded function on a bounded set. Then the following are equivalent:

    1. $f$ is integrable on $A$:
      $$ \overline{\int_A} f = \underline{\int_A} f (= I) $$
    2. $f$ satisfies the Riemann condition:
      For all $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists a partition $P_\varepsilon$ (of $B$) s.t. $$ 0 \leq U(f, P_\varepsilon) - L(f, P_\varepsilon) < \varepsilon $$
    3. $f$ satisfies Darboux’s condition:
      For every number $I$ that has the following property:
      $\forall \varepsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0$, such that for any partition $P = { B_1, B_2, \dots, B_N }$ with side length $< \delta$, we have $$ \geq\left| \sum_{i=1}^{N} f(x_i)V(B_i) - I \right| < \varepsilon $$ for any choice of $x_i \in B_i$, $i = 1, \dots, N$.

    I*. Proof of the Equivalence (continued) #

    Proof of (1) $\Leftrightarrow$ (2): Done!

    Step 3: #

    Prove (3) $\Rightarrow$ (2):

    Note: Darboux’s condition $\Rightarrow$ integrability.
    Let $I$ be the number in condition (3).
    Want to show: $\overline{\int_A} f = \underline{\int_A} f = \int_A f$

    [!claim] $\forall \varepsilon > 0$, $\exists$ partition $P$ s.t. $$ \boxed{|L(f, P) - I| < \varepsilon}\quad(*) $$

    We want to show that: $$\begin{align} L(f, p) - I & = \Big| L(f, p) - \sum f(x_i)V(h_i) \Big|

    • \Big| \sum f(x_i)V(h_i) - I \Big| \ & = \sum \Big|\inf_{h_i} f - f(x_i)\Big| V(h_i)
    • \frac{\varepsilon}{2} \ & < \sum_{i=1}^{N}\frac{\frac{\varepsilon}{2}}{2V(B_i)} \cdot V(h_i) \end{align}$$

    Given $\varepsilon > 0$, by (3), $\exists , \delta > 0$ s.t. for any partition $$ P = {B_1, \dots, B_N}\quad \text{with sides} < \delta $$ we have $$ \left|\sum_{i=1}^{N} f(x_i)V(B_i) - I\right| < \frac{\varepsilon}{2} $$

    for any $x_i \in B_i$.

    To prove $(*)$, we can choose $x_i \in B_i$ s.t. $$ 0 \leq f(x_i) - \inf_{B_i} f(x) < \frac{\varepsilon}{2N,V(B_i)} $$

    Then it follows that $$ |L(f,p)-I| \leq |L(f,p)-\sum_i f(x_i)V(B_i)| + |\sum_i f(x_i)V(B_i)-I| $$ $$ \leq \sum_{i=1}^{N}|\inf_{B_i} f(x)-f(x_i)|\cdot V(B_i) + \frac{\varepsilon}{2} $$ $$ < \sum_{i=1}^{N}\frac{\varepsilon}{2N,V(B_i)}\cdot V(B_i) + \frac{\varepsilon}{2} $$ $$ = \frac{\varepsilon}{2}+\frac{\varepsilon}{2} = \varepsilon \quad \Longrightarrow (*) $$

    Furthermore, $(*)\Rightarrow$ $$ L(f,p)>I-\varepsilon\quad \forall \varepsilon >0. $$ Thus, $$ \left( \underline{\int}A f = \sup{P}L(f,p)\geq I \right). $$

    Similarly, $\forall \varepsilon>0$, $\exists P$ s.t. $$ |U(f,p)-I|<\varepsilon $$ $$ \Longrightarrow \overline{\int}A f = \inf{P}U(f,p)\leq I $$

    Step 4 (1) ⇒ (3) (Ideas) #

    • Given $\varepsilon > 0$, $\exists$ partition $P$ such that

    $$ I - \frac{\varepsilon}{2} < L(f,P) \leq \sum_j f(x_j)V(b_j) \leq U(f,P) < I + \frac{\varepsilon}{2} $$

    • Given partition $P$, $\exists \delta > 0$ such that for any partition $P’$ with side length < $\delta$, the sum of volumes of the subrectangles in $P’$ that are not entirely contained in a subrectangle in $P$ is less than $\varepsilon$.

    II Criterion for Integrability #

    Question: When is $f$ integrable?

    (How can we tell from other properties?)

    Answer: It is integrable when the set of discontinuity is “small”.

    Measuring the Size of a Set #

    (a) Volume of $A$ #

    Given a bounded set $A \subset \mathbb{R}^n$, define the characteristic function of $A$ by:

    $$ 1_A(x) = \begin{cases} 1, & x \in A \ 0, & x \notin A \end{cases} $$

    We say $A$ has volume (or is measurable) if $1_A(x)$ is integrable on $A$. Then, the volume is given by:

    $$ V(A) = \int_A 1_A(x),dx $$

    Remark:

    • If $n = 1$, then $V(A)$ represents “length.”
    • If $n = 2$, then $V(A)$ represents “area”.

    (b) “Small” Sets #

    These are sets of measure zero, defined as sets for which, given any $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists a finite covering by rectangles (or intervals) whose total volume is less than $\varepsilon$. Formally:

    $$ \text{For every } \varepsilon > 0, \text{ there exist sets } S_1, S_2, \dots, S_N \text{ such that } A \subset \bigcup_{i=1}^{N} S_i \text{ and } \sum_{i=1}^{N} V(S_i) < \varepsilon. $$

    Proof of implication $(\Rightarrow)$: #

    Suppose $V(A) = \int_A 1_A(x),dx = 0$. Then, for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists a partition $P = {P_j}$ covering the set $A$ such that:

    $$ U(1_A(x), P) < \varepsilon $$

    Thus, selecting all sets $P_j$ where $P_j \cap A \neq \emptyset$, we obtain a finite covering of $A$ satisfying:

    $$ \sum_{P_j \cap A \ne \emptyset} V(P_j) < \varepsilon $$

    Generalization of Measure Zero #

    [!definition] A set $A \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ (not necessarily bounded) is said to have measure zero if, for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists a countable covering of $A$ by rectangles ${S_i}$ satisfying: $$\sum_{i=1}^{\infty} V(S_i) < \varepsilon $$

    Remarks:

    • If $V(A) = 0$, then it implies measure $m(A) = 0$.
    • Any finite set has volume zero.
    • Any countable set has measure zero.
